Brand Specification

This brand guide features information on WonderTax Labs brand policy and logo usage guidelines.

The WonderTax Labs Brand

WonderTax Labs believes in integrity, transparency, and professionalism. We value and respect our team members, platform partners, and customers. We take pride in our code, accountability, and teamwork—to foster a community of respect, acceptance, and trust, believing "teamwork makes the dream work."1

This document explains how best to use our WonderTax Labs brand name and logo. We also have this brand specification available as a PDF.

We encourage you to read this document carefully before using our brand name or logo. Your cooperation is highly appreciated!

The WonderTax Labs Wordmark

  1. "WonderTax Labs" should be used solely to refer to our company.
  2. "WonderTax Labs" is a singular entity. Although the word mark ends with an "s" (suggesting plural), it is still singular.
  3. WonderTax Labs has a registered United States Trademark on the word "WonderTax" (registration number: 90056834). As such, you must include the Registered Trademark symbol (®) upon first use in any publication to sufficiently put the public on notice of our registered trademark. The registered trademark symbol does not need to be repeated in subsequent use in the same publication.
  4. If possible, we prefer the ® symbol to be set in superscript (in HTML: <sup>&reg;</sup>).
  5. Care should be taken such that the word "Labs" is not auto-wrapped to a next line, visually seperated from "WonderTax." Compliance with this requirement may require surgical/tactical use of CSS white-space: nowrap; or strategic use of HTML ENTITY &nbsp; between the words "WonderTax" and "Labs."
  6. Our registered trademark is on the term "WonderTax" — not on the longer "WonderTax Labs"

The WonderTax Labs Brand Palette

"On" colors

WonderTax Labs has adopted color palette logic developed by Google's Material Design 3 specification, to ensure contrast and readability.

App surfaces use colors from specific categories in [our brand] color palette, such as a primary color. Whenever elements, such as text or icons, appear in front of those surfaces, those elements should use colors designed to be clear and legible against the colors behind them.

This category of colors is called “on” colors, referring to the fact that they color elements that appear “on” top of surfaces that use the following colors: a primary color, secondary color, surface color, background color, or error color. When a color appears “on” top of a primary color, it’s called an “on primary color.” They are labelled using the original color category (such as primary color) with the prefix “on.”

“On” colors are primarily applied to text, iconography, and strokes. Sometimes, they are applied to surfaces.
